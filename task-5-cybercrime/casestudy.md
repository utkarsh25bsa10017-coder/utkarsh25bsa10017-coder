

Case Study: The "Accidental Transfer" UPI Scam
What the Crime Is:
UPI (Unified Payments Interface) fraud frequently relies on social engineering rather than complex technical hacking. In this scenario, a fraudster manipulates a victim into willingly transferring funds by creating a false narrative and a sense of extreme urgency.

How It Happens (Step-by-Step):

The Setup: A college student receives an SMS that perfectly mimics their bank’s official alert format, falsely stating that ₹10,000 has been credited to their account.

The Hook: Minutes later, a panicked stranger calls the student. The caller claims they mistakenly transferred their college fee to the student's number by typing one wrong digit on Google Pay.

The Manipulation: The scammer begs the student to return the funds immediately, creating high emotional pressure and exploiting the student's empathy.

The Execution: Distracted and rushed, the student relies on the fake SMS instead of opening their actual banking app to verify the balance. They use their UPI app to send ₹10,000 back to the caller.

The Realization: The student later checks their official bank statement and realizes the initial credit never happened.

Who is Usually Targeted:
This scam heavily targets college students, busy professionals, and the elderly—demographics likely to act quickly out of empathy or distraction without verifying facts.

Consequences:
The immediate consequence is sudden financial loss. Because the victim actively entered their UPI PIN to authorize the transfer, banks usually cannot reverse the transaction, leaving the victim with almost no way to recover the stolen money.
